## 1. Instagram Reels Algorithm — What's Confirmed for 2025-2026

This section covers only what is verified beyond the base file. General signals are in `instagram_reels_best_practices_2025.md`.

### Mosseri's January 2025 Confirmed Signals (ranked)
Source: Adam Mosseri public statement, January 2025. Cited independently by Dataslayer, Serotonin Digital, Buffer, and Hootsuite.

1. **Watch time** — Most important. Both relative (% completion) and absolute (seconds) matter. The 3-second threshold is the first distribution gate.
2. **Sends per reach (DM shares)** — Most important for reaching *new* audiences (unconnected reach). Mosseri's quote via podcast hosts Colin and Samir: *"Short-form video is so symbiotic with connecting people with their friends."*
3. **Likes per reach** — Matters more for connected reach (existing followers) than for new audience discovery.

### December 2025 Update: "Your Algorithm" Feature
Source: TechCrunch, December 10 2025; ABC News; Fast Company.

Instagram launched "Your Algorithm" on December 10, 2025, giving users a visible list of what topics Instagram thinks they care about, and allowing them to add/remove topics. This matters for dermatology because:
- If a user has "skincare" or "dermatology" in their topic list, they receive more of that content in their Reels tab.
- Topic classification is now explicit — dermatology Reels should signal their topic clearly in the first 3 seconds (visuals + text overlay) to be correctly classified.
- Rolling out globally in English as of early 2026.

### Original Content Penalty for Reposts
Source: Multiple sources converging on same data point (Stackinfluence, Creatorflow, Buffer).

Instagram detects cross-posting from other platforms. Content retaining 70%+ original visual/audio elements flagged as repost. Accounts posting 10+ reposts within 30 days are excluded from Reels recommendations entirely.

**Implication for dermatology:** Cross-posting from TikTok (common advice) will result in reach suppression if the TikTok watermark is visible. Remove watermarks before cross-posting or shoot natively for Instagram.

### Saves as Secondary Signal
Source: Multiple 2025 marketing research sources.

Saves are interpreted as "user wants to come back to this" — high-value signal for educational content. Carousel posts (static) generate more saves than Reels on average, but educational Reels (skincare routines, condition explanations) drive save behavior when they contain reference-worthy information.

**Practical implication:** End educational Reels with "Save this for your next consultation" — this CTA has both behavioral and algorithmic benefit.

### Instagram vs Twitter/X — Key Algorithmic Difference
Source: Tweetarchivist.com comparison, Stackinfluence.

| Dimension | Instagram Reels | Twitter/X |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Primary reach signal | DM shares + watch time | Replies (most valued), then retweets, likes weakest |
| New audience discovery | Via Explore + Reels tab algorithm | Via For You feed, replies to viral threads |
| Content longevity | Reels continue distributing for days/weeks if signal holds | Tweets decay in 24-48 hours |
| Original content | Strongly rewarded, cross-posting penalized | No cross-platform penalty detected |
| User base (2025) | 2.35B MAU | 586M MAU |
| Engagement character | Visual, saves, DM-shares | Text-based, reply threads |

**Critical difference:** On Twitter, replies drive distribution. On Instagram, DM shares drive distribution. Content designed to spark conversation (reply bait) works for Twitter; content designed to be forwarded to a friend (shareable, useful, surprising) works for Instagram Reels.

### Falsifiability
If Instagram publishes internal weighting documentation showing completion rate outranks DM sends at all stages of distribution, the send-first content design recommendation would need revision.

## 2. Top Dermatology Accounts — Verified Data

### Academic-Sourced Account Data
Source: Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology (JAAD), PubMed ID 33988555, "Increasing a Dermatologist's Footprint on Instagram: Analysis of Top Influencers Performing Nonsurgical Cosmetic Procedures"

Study analyzed 99 influencers posting about nonsurgical cosmetic procedures.

Key findings:
- Most popular posts were **before-and-after photographs and personal posts**
- 70% of the top 10 were board-certified plastic surgeons
- Physicians lacking board certification in cosmetic specialties achieved the **highest influencer ratings** — interpreted as the "relatability gap" where accessible presentation outperforms formal credential signaling in the algorithm
- Dermatologists who posted before/after + personal content consistently outperformed those who posted only educational or clinical content

**Note:** Study data is from 2021. The algorithm has changed significantly since then (DM sends were not yet confirmed as the top signal). Before/after as organic content type likely still holds — the format has survived multiple algorithm changes — but the specific engagement numbers are not transferable to 2025.

## 3. Content Formats — What Works for Dermatology

### Format Performance Ranking (synthesized from multiple sources)

Evidence levels: HIGH = multiple sources + some data backing; MEDIUM = multiple sources, qualitative only; LOW = single source or inference.

| Format | Evidence Level | Mechanism | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-----------|-------|
| Before/After transformation | HIGH | Visual proof of result; clear narrative arc; completion incentive (viewers want to see the reveal) | Subject to HWG restrictions in Germany — see Section 5 |
| "Dermatologist reacts to viral trend" | MEDIUM | Borrowed authority from trending content; positions doctor as arbiter; comment-triggering | Example: responding when dangerous DIY peels go viral |
| Procedure close-up (oddly satisfying) | MEDIUM | Replay behavior (pimple extraction, laser, chemical peel). Multiple replays = strong algo signal | Dr. Pimple Popper's core format |
| Myth-busting ("You've been doing X wrong") | MEDIUM | Contrarian hook. High save rate (reference content). Comment debate driver | Works best with specific, falsifiable claims |
| Educational step-by-step (skincare routine) | MEDIUM | High save rate. Lower shareability than myth-busting | Drives "save" behavior; best for patient retention, not new audience discovery |
| Patient reveal / reaction | MEDIUM | Emotional peak; comment and tag-a-friend behavior. High DM share potential | Requires patient consent. HIPAA (US) / DSGVO (Germany) compliance required |
| "Things I'd never let my [family] do as a dermatologist" | LOW | Expert + relatable personal angle. Borrowed from dental Dr. Burhenne format | No dermatology-specific data; transferred from dental category |
| Treatment demo (procedure in action) | MEDIUM | "How it works" curiosity. Educational + trust building | Works with voiceover explaining; silent versions lose engagement |
| Skin condition explainer | MEDIUM | High search relevance (condition-related hashtags); save behavior | Longer format (30-45s) appropriate here |

### Engagement Benchmark for Dermatology Reels
Source: PMC article PMC10334950, cross-sectional study of dermatology residency Instagram accounts.

Instagram Reels posts from dermatology accounts achieved:
- Engagement rate range: 0.09% - 3.45%
- Median engagement rate: 1.17%

**Context:** This study measured residency program accounts, not individual practitioner influencer accounts. Individual dermatologist influencer accounts (Dr. Pimple Popper, Dr. Zubritsky) likely have higher engagement rates due to personal branding. The 1.17% median is a floor, not a ceiling, for a well-run practice account.

### What Would Disprove
Engagement rate data from a 2024-2025 study of dermatology accounts showing a different content format (not before/after or procedure close-up) as the top performer.

## 4. Technical Specifications — Dermatology-Specific Notes

General technical specs (resolution, safe zones, duration, file format) are in `instagram_reels_best_practices_2025.md`. Only dermatology-specific notes here.

### Optimal Duration by Dermatology Content Type
Source: Synthesis from multiple marketing sources + general Reels engagement data.

| Content type | Recommended duration | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Before/After reveal | 7-15s | Single narrative arc, no padding needed |
| Procedure close-up (oddly satisfying) | 7-15s | Loop behavior; shorter = more replays |
| Myth-busting | 15-30s | Needs setup (myth) + payoff (truth) + evidence |
| Skin condition explainer | 30-45s | Complex topic needs adequate time; must have clear narrative to hold completion |
| Treatment Q&A / FAQ | 30-60s | Information density justifies length if each beat is tight |
| Patient journey/testimonial | 45-60s | Storytelling justifies length; patient voice is the engagement hook |

**General rule confirmed across sources:** If there is no clear narrative reason for the length, cut to the shorter version. Algorithm rewards completion rate, not information completeness.

### Caption Strategy for Medical Practices
Source: Multiple practitioner sources, base file.

Medical captions should include:
1. Hook line (visible before "more" click) — this is the content hook, not the medical disclaimer
2. 2-3 lines of educational value or context
3. One CTA only
4. 3-5 hashtags (not 20-25 — Instagram's official Creators account recommends 3-5; the "43% more reach with hashtags" figure is unverified as to optimal count)
5. Medical disclaimer, if required — put at the END of the caption, not the opening line

**Hashtag debate:** Sources conflict. Instagram's own Creators account recommends 3-5. Third-party studies cite ranges from 5-25. The "43% more reach" figure for Reels with hashtags is widely cited but the original study is not attributed. Use 5-7 targeted hashtags for medical practices until stronger data is available.

### Posting Frequency
Source: Multiple dermatology marketing sources.

Recommended range: 3-7 posts per week. Multiple sources cite 3-5 as a sustainable target for a practice.

**Confidence: LOW.** No controlled study on dermatology practice accounts was found comparing posting frequencies against engagement or follower growth. These are marketing blog recommendations, not data-backed optima.

Key caveat: Consistency outperforms frequency. An account that posts 3x/week reliably will outperform one that posts 7x/week for two weeks and then stops.

## 5. Germany — HWG Compliance for Dermatology Reels

This section is the highest-confidence section in this document. Sources are traceable to specific court rulings.

### Finding 1: Before/After Advertising for Cosmetic Procedures is Prohibited

**Source:** German Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof, BGH), ruling dated July 31, 2025.
**Secondary source:** Library of Congress, Global Legal Monitor, published September 25, 2025.
**URL:** https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-09-25/germany-federal-court-of-justice-rules-that-before-and-after-ads-for-cosmetic-treatments-are-prohibited

**The ruling:** Before-and-after images in advertising for plastic and cosmetic treatments violates the Heilmittelwerbegesetz (HWG) — Germany's Health Services and Products Advertising Act.

**Explicitly covered:** Hyaluronic acid injections and hyaluronidase injections are classified as "operative plastic surgical procedures" under HWG, even though they are non-surgical in the clinical sense. The BGH rejected the argument that only traditional surgery falls under the statute.

**The legal logic:** The court interpreted the HWG list of procedures as non-exhaustive. The test is whether the procedure alters the human body and is performed on humans. Hyaluronic acid injections meet this test.

**Not covered (confirmed by ruling):** Ear piercings, body piercings, tattooing. The court explicitly excluded these from the "operative plastic surgical interventions" category.

**Preu Bohlig legal analysis (law firm):** The prohibition extends to advertising using avatars — a virtual "before/after" representation of the procedure result is also prohibited. Source: https://preubohlig.de/newsletter/before-and-after-advertising-for-cosmetic-surgery-also-prohibited-with-avatar/?lang=en

**Lexology analysis:** The ban covers "aesthetic medicine" broadly — this includes procedures like fillers, Botox, laser treatments, and chemical peels when performed for cosmetic purposes.

**What counts as "advertising":** The HWG applies to promotional communication. Organic social media posts that showcase results with implicit or explicit promotional intent are treated as advertising under German law. The "it's just organic content" defense has not protected practices in enforcement cases.

**CRITICAL IMPLICATION FOR INSTAGRAM REELS:** A dermatology practice with a German audience cannot show before/after cosmetic procedure images in Reels, even in organic posts, if those posts have promotional intent. "Before and after" as a Reel format — the most consistently high-performing format — is legally restricted for the German market for a wide range of cosmetic procedures.

### Finding 2: Pharmaceutical Advertising via Influencers Requires Mandatory HWG Warning

**Source:** German court ruling, case involving an 18-second Reel, reported December 2025.
**Secondary source:** GlobalPolicyWatch, December 2025.
**URL:** https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2025/12/influencer-marketing-german-court-sets-new-guardrails-for-pharmaceutical-advertising-on-social-media/

**The ruling:** When a Reel is used to advertise a medicinal product (OTC or otherwise), the mandatory HWG warning notice must appear in the video itself — not just in a caption or description.

**Influencer as "known person":** Influencers with niche followings (the case involved 130,000 followers) can qualify as "known persons" under HWG even without universal celebrity status. This matters because HWG restricts certain forms of celebrity endorsement for pharmaceuticals.

**Company liability:** The practice or company commissioning the Reel content can be held directly liable for compliance failures by the influencer, even when they did not create the content themselves.

**Applies to:** Advertising for OTC medicines. Prescription-only medicines cannot be advertised to the general public at all (per HWG and EU Directive 2001/83/EC).

### Meta Advertising Policy (Separate from HWG)

**Source:** Meta Transparency Center, shop2app.com analysis, multiple legal/marketing sources.

Meta's own advertising policies (which apply regardless of national law) prohibit the following in paid ads:
- Before-and-after depictions must reflect realistic outcomes over time; side-by-side comparisons in ads are not allowed
- Ads must not imply negative self-perception to drive action
- Close-ups of aging skin, wrinkles, or other signs of aging are not allowed in ads
- Cosmetic ads must target users 18+ only

**Key distinction:** These restrictions apply to Meta paid advertising, not to organic posts. Organic posts showing before/after results are NOT restricted by Meta's ad policies. The HWG restriction (above) operates independently of Meta's platform policy and applies based on German law regardless of whether the content is paid or organic.

**2025 healthcare advertising change:** Meta classified health and wellness brands as "sensitive categories" in early 2025, limiting how advertisers use data for targeting and tracking. Campaigns can no longer target users based on health conditions or behaviors tied to medical data. This is a targeting restriction, not a content restriction.

### Compliance Summary Table for German Dermatology Practice

| Content type | Organic Reel | Paid Reel Ad | German Law Applies? |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Before/after cosmetic procedure | PROHIBITED under HWG (organic posts with promo intent) | PROHIBITED under both HWG and Meta policy | YES |
| Before/after non-cosmetic / medical condition documentation | REVIEW REQUIRED | PROHIBITED in ads per Meta | Likely yes if "advertising" |
| Educational skincare content (no before/after) | Permitted | Permitted with targeting restrictions | HWG generally not triggered |
| OTC product promotion | Permitted with HWG warning in video | Restricted targeting; HWG warning required | YES |
| Prescription product promotion | PROHIBITED to general public | PROHIBITED | YES |
| Procedure explainer (no result claims) | Permitted | Permitted with targeting restrictions | Low risk if no outcome claims |
| Patient testimonial / reaction video | REVIEW REQUIRED (testimonials can be advertising) | PROHIBITED per HWG | YES |

**Note:** "REVIEW REQUIRED" means consult a German healthcare advertising attorney. This table is research synthesis, not legal advice.

### Falsifiability
If a German court rules that organic Instagram posts by a dermatology practice do not constitute "advertising" under HWG when no explicit booking/purchase CTA is present, the restriction on before/after organic posts would be narrowed. The current legal interpretation is that promotional intent is sufficient — but this has not been tested extensively for organic social media posts specifically (as opposed to paid ads or influencer partnerships).

## 7. Shared Assumptions Across Sources (Downstream Agent Flag)

These assumptions appear across multiple sources without being stated. If any breaks, multiple findings collapse.

1. **Before/after prohibition (HWG) applies uniformly.** The BGH ruling covers hyaluronic acid injections. Application to other cosmetic procedures (e.g., laser, chemical peels, Botox) is based on legal analysis by law firms (Preu Bohlig, Lexology), not yet a BGH ruling on those specific procedures. The ruling's logic extends them — but it is an inference, not a second ruling.

2. **Meta's "organic posts are unrestricted" still holds.** Meta has changed its advertising policies multiple times in 2025. The distinction between organic and paid content could be narrowed by a future policy update. Verify before building a content strategy that relies on this distinction.

3. **Algorithm signals from general Reels data transfer to dermatology content.** All algorithm findings (DM sends, watch time, completion rate) are from studies of general Reels, not specifically medical/dermatology content. Health content may behave differently due to topic sensitivity classification.

4. **Follower count = influence.** The account size data (Shah, Zubritsky, Lee) reflects cumulative audience building, not current Reel performance. A large account with declining engagement may perform worse than a small, high-engagement account. Per-Reel engagement data was not obtainable.

## 8. Alternative Interpretations

### Finding: Before/after is the top-performing organic content type for dermatology

**Reading A (dominant):** Before/after works because it provides visual proof of results, creates a satisfying narrative arc (setup → transformation → reveal), and drives saves and shares. Build content strategy around this format.

**Reading B:** Before/after performs well in follower count and like metrics, but these are vanity metrics. A before/after Reel may attract watchers from the general public who would never book the procedure, inflating engagement without driving bookings. Educational content about specific conditions may attract a narrower audience with higher booking intent. For a practice that wants new patients, not just reach, educational content may convert better despite lower raw engagement.

Weight to assign: unclear. Both can be true simultaneously — before/after for top-of-funnel reach, educational content for bottom-of-funnel conversion.

### Finding: HWG before/after prohibition is strict and broad

**Reading A:** The BGH ruling is unambiguous. Before/after for cosmetic procedures is prohibited. Dermatology practices targeting German audiences must remove before/after format from their Reels strategy entirely.

**Reading B:** The ruling covers "advertising" — a term that implies promotional intent. A practice could argue that an organic educational post documenting a treatment outcome (not intended to solicit business) does not constitute advertising. This argument has not been tested in court for Instagram Reels specifically. A practice with legal counsel and documented non-commercial intent for educational posts might operate in this gray zone.

Weight to assign: Reading A is the legally safe interpretation. Reading B is a risk argument that requires specialist legal advice.

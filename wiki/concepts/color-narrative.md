# Color Narrative

> Color is not decoration or mood — it is a narrative layer that encodes the emotional arc of a story across time. The shift FROM one color state TO another IS a plot event. Pixar color scripts, warm/cool psychology, saturation as arousal, and color arcs across sequences.

## Core Findings

### Pixar's Color Script Technique

A color script is a sequential series of paintings — small-format, quick studies — that map out the emotional arc of an entire film before a single frame is animated. Each panel represents a key scene or story beat. Together they show how color, light, saturation, and temperature evolve across the runtime. [VERIFIED]

Ralph Eggleston brought color scripts to Pixar for Toy Story (1995), working in pastel for speed and efficiency. Every Pixar film since has had one. [VERIFIED]

The purpose is explicitly narrative, not aesthetic. Directors use color scripts to ensure the audience feels the emotional weight of each beat without being told. The key principle: if the animator maintains consistent color-emotion relationships throughout the film, the audience subconsciously connects palettes with emotion without conscious recognition. [VERIFIED]

**Documented examples:**
- **Up (opening sequence):** Carl Fredricksen's palette centers on warm browns to signal grief rooted in memory and attachment. [VERIFIED]
- **Coco (Land of the Dead):** Crosses into the Land of the Dead and "cranks up all the dials, colorwise." Scenes described as "neon, like a bio-organic version of Tokyo's Shinjuku District." Luminous orange marigolds are the connective tissue — the color of memory and crossing over. [VERIFIED]
- **Inside Out (Sadness):** White in Sadness's palette is enhanced with blue-adjacent, desaturated tones per scene to deepen the emotional signal. [THEORETICAL]
- **Joker:** Progresses from muted greens and grays to bolder, high-saturation colors as Arthur Fleck descends into madness. Color becomes louder as the character loses control. [VERIFIED]

**Pre-animation constraint:** Because computer animation is nonlinear in production, the director needs the color script as a reference to ensure the emotional temperature of any scene matches where it falls in the arc. Implication for web/brand film: build a color script before building scenes, or risk locally acceptable but emotionally incoherent sequences. [VERIFIED]

### The Foundational Warm/Cool Split

This is the most consistently documented finding across all sources. [VERIFIED]

| Color Temperature | Kelvin Equivalent | Emotional Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Warm (oranges, reds, amber) | 2,000K-3,500K | Safety, intimacy, belonging, nostalgia |
| Neutral (daylight) | 5,000K-5,500K | Normalcy, reality, no emotional loading |
| Cool (blues, blue-greens) | 6,500K-10,000K+ | Isolation, tension, dread, clinical sterility |

**Documented film examples:**
- **Her (2013):** Warm reds, golds, oranges throughout. Signal: emotional intimacy and vulnerability between human and AI. [VERIFIED]
- **The Revenant:** Predominantly cool temperature. Mirrors protagonist's isolation in hostile landscape. [VERIFIED]
- **Harry Potter:** Interior Hogwarts uses warm amber (2,800K) for "finally home." The Deathly Hallows shifts to desaturated blue-gray for bleakness as Voldemort's power rises. [VERIFIED]

### The Saturation Axis

Saturation and hue are separate variables that filmmakers use independently. [VERIFIED]

| Saturation Level | Emotional Signal | Physiological Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Highly desaturated (near grayscale) | Depression, boredom, passivity | Lower arousal, lower skin conductance |
| Moderately desaturated | Calm, introspective, character-focused | Moderate |
| Neutral saturation | Realism, credibility | Neutral |
| High saturation | Energy, excitement, climax, chaos | Measurably higher skin conductance (physical excitement) |
| Oversaturated | Overwhelming, nauseating, surreal | Overstimulation |

Key finding: "Desaturation tends to emphasize the character (by focusing the viewer); saturation tends to emphasize the world by broadening the field of view." [VERIFIED]

Physiological evidence: Saturated and bright colors produce significantly stronger skin conductance responses — the body physically reacts more intensely to vivid color. This is measurable, not subjective. [VERIFIED — PMC article 10867063]

### The Color Arc: Color Mood vs. Color Arc

**Color mood:** "This scene is warm and safe." (Static.) [VERIFIED]

**Color arc:** "This film starts warm and bleeds into cold as the character loses everything, then recovers warmth in the final act." (Dynamic — the change encodes the arc.) [VERIFIED]

A color arc works by assigning a color state to a narrative state, then shifting between them in sync with the story. The viewer feels the arc before they understand it.

**Case studies:**
- **Mad Max: Fury Road:** Desert = extreme gold/orange (relentless, oppressive). Night = extreme blue (relief from gold). Escape = golden protagonists against white sky. The green place = deepest blues — as far from orange as possible — visual turning point before the script says it. Arc: Gold (captivity) to Blue (different world) to Gold again (return, but changed). [VERIFIED]
- **Her:** Warm pastels, reds and pinks shift to cooler blues and greens as emotional isolation grows. Warmth is not happiness — it is tenderness + vulnerability. Cool is not sadness — it is disconnection. Arc: Warmth (connection possible) to Cool (connection severed). [VERIFIED]

### The Manchester Color Wheel: Clinical Evidence

The Manchester Color Wheel study (n=323: 105 healthy, 108 anxious, 110 depressed) is the most rigorous clinical source. [VERIFIED — PMC article 2829580]

- **Healthy mood maps to Yellow** (specifically "Yellow 14"). Yellow was the most-chosen "mood color" for healthy participants.
- **Anxious/depressed mood maps to Grey** ("Grey 35" and "Grey 36" dominated for anxious and depressed participants).
- Yellow choice is significantly reduced in both depressed (p<0.001) and anxious (p=0.001) individuals vs healthy controls.
- "Depressed people feel more gray than blue."

Design implication: desaturated gray is clinically associated with low-mood states. The transition FROM gray/cool-desaturated TO warm-yellow-green is a literal color journey from anxiety to relief. This maps directly to what clinically anxious people report feeling. [VERIFIED source; design implication is THEORETICAL]

### Beat-Boundary vs. Continuous Gradient Transitions

Film evidence shows two transition types serve different functions: [VERIFIED]

**Continuous gradient:** Subconscious emotional carrier. Gradual changes "fly under the radar of perception until a smooth change in mood is achieved." Used within a scene for mood maintenance. [VERIFIED]

**Beat-boundary shift:** Conscious narrative signal. Abrupt color shifts mark story beat boundaries and serve dramatic/comedic purpose. The shift is punctuation — it signals "something has changed." [VERIFIED]

**The hybrid model (recommended):** Neither approach alone is optimal. Use continuous gradient between beats (mood carrier) and beat-boundary shift at key narrative moments (punctuation). The gradient carries the audience toward the beat; the beat-boundary delivers the emotional punch. [THEORETICAL]

This is the approach NRK uses in scroll-driven journalism: continuous environmental changes carry the reader between hard narrative beats. [VERIFIED]

### Scroll-Driven Color Arcs

Apple and NRK are the best-documented examples of brands using scroll-driven background color transitions as narrative. [VERIFIED]

- **NRK:** Background visuals animate continuously with scroll. Night falls progressively, lights turn on, color temperature shifts. Story told without text. [VERIFIED]
- **Apple:** Product pages use cinematic scrolling. Background shifts from deep black to product-appropriate tones as features scroll in. [THEORETICAL]

The CSS Scroll-driven Animations API (`animation-timeline: scroll()`) enables native synchronization of color transitions to scroll position without JavaScript. Production-ready in 2025 Chromium and Safari. [VERIFIED]

### Color Grammar: The Emotional Dictionary

| Color | Encodes | Counter-encodes |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Warm reds / oranges | Threat, energy, passion, urgency | Safety through familiarity, appetite |
| Deep blues | Calm, cold, isolation, distance | Authority, precision, trust |
| Greens | Growth, nature, health | Sickness (wrong shade), alien |
| Gold / amber | Power, warmth, nostalgia | Stagnation, trapped in time |
| White / bright whites | Clarity, purity, space | Emptiness, clinical detachment |
| Muted / desaturated | Reality, constraint, depression | Sophistication, restraint |
| High saturation | Intensity, heightened reality | Synthetic, false, overwhelming |
| Low saturation + one saturated element | Isolation of what matters | The rest is noise |

## Operational Rules

1. **When planning any sequence with emotional arc (animation, scroll experience, brand film), create a color script BEFORE building scenes.** Map every emotional beat to a color state. Treat the color transition as a script element, not a styling decision. [VERIFIED]

2. **When building a dark-to-warm arc, use hue rotation + saturation increase while keeping lightness relatively constant.** The background should become warm-dark rather than light-warm. This preserves dark aesthetics while traversing the emotional arc. Hue rotates from blue-dark (~HSL 220) to green-dark (~HSL 150), roughly 70 degrees. [THEORETICAL]

3. **When choosing between continuous gradient and beat-boundary shift, apply the rule: gradients carry mood subconsciously, beat-boundaries punctuate consciously.** Use beat-boundary only when the emotional state changes category (confusion to relief). Use continuous within a category (confusion deepening). [VERIFIED]

4. **When encoding arousal or intensity, manipulate saturation first, hue second.** Saturation is the strongest predictor of physiological arousal. High saturation produces measurably stronger skin conductance responses. Hue has a secondary effect. [VERIFIED]

5. **When the color arc must work in compressed time (8-second brand film vs. 90-minute feature), make beat-boundary shifts more decisive and faster.** The pace of emotional travel must be compressed; film precedent may need to be overridden. [THEORETICAL]

6. **When implementing scroll-driven color transitions, use `animation-timeline: scroll()` in CSS for native synchronization.** This avoids JavaScript overhead and is production-ready in modern browsers. [VERIFIED]

7. **When testing the legibility of a color arc, ask: would someone watching without audio register that the emotional temperature changed at the right moments?** Color arcs that require audio or text to be legible are failing. The visual arc should be independently readable. [THEORETICAL]

## Open Questions

- Most film and design color psychology research is conducted with Western audiences. Color associations are not fully universal. Warm colors signal danger in some cultural contexts.
- Pixar color scripts work over 90 minutes. Brand films may be 8 seconds. Whether color arc techniques at this compressed timescale produce the same emotional effect is unverified.
- The Manchester Color Wheel study was UK-based. Replication in different cultural contexts has not been confirmed.
- The dark-to-warm arc interpretation could be read as "danger to warmth" (threat-safety) rather than "confusion to clarity" — both are plausible. Which reading dominates has not been tested.
- No direct head-to-head study comparing beat-boundary vs. continuous gradient effectiveness in web UX contexts was found.

# Emotion, Perception, and Neuroscience

> How the human brain processes visual information emotionally: pre-attentive processing in under 200ms, amygdala threat detection at 88ms, looming motion capture, color-emotion mappings driven by saturation and lightness more than hue, the peak-end rule for experience memory, and the neurochemistry of sharing behavior. These are the biological constraints that any visual design operates within.

## Core Findings

### Pre-Attentive Processing (<200ms)

The human visual system processes information in two phases. The first -- pre-attentive processing -- occurs in under 200 milliseconds, before conscious awareness or voluntary eye movement. [VERIFIED -- Treisman & Gelade, 1980, Cognitive Psychology]

**What the brain detects pre-attentively:** Color (hue), orientation, size, curvature, length, intensity, motion, and depth of field. These features are extracted in parallel across the entire visual field without requiring focused attention. Anne Treisman's Feature Integration Theory (1980) established that only the *binding* of multiple features into a coherent object requires focused attention; individual features are detected automatically. [VERIFIED -- peer-reviewed]

**Practical consequence:** The first 200ms of any visual frame is processed before the viewer is consciously aware of what they are looking at. Compositional choices about color, contrast, and motion are not aesthetic preferences -- they are instructions to the pre-attentive system. [VERIFIED]

### Amygdala Threat Detection (88ms)

The amygdala begins responding to emotionally threatening faces within 88 milliseconds of stimulus onset, even when the stimulus is invisible (presented below conscious awareness through binocular suppression). Ensemble responses of amygdala neurons carry robust information about threatening (open-mouthed) faces within 50ms. This happens via a subcortical pathway that bypasses the cortex entirely, conveying crude but rapid signals before conscious awareness. [VERIFIED -- Journal of Neuroscience, 2023; Cerebral Cortex, 2022]

**Emotion enhances perception itself:** Phelps, Ling, and Carrasco (2006) demonstrated that emotion enhances contrast sensitivity independently of attention, and that emotion potentiates the effect of attention on contrast sensitivity. Emotional content does not merely attract attention -- it literally makes you see better. [VERIFIED -- Psychological Science, 2006]

### Looming Motion Capture

Objects moving toward the observer automatically capture visuospatial attention, even without conscious awareness. This is the single most attention-capturing stimulus type. [VERIFIED -- Franconeri & Simons, 2003, Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics]

Key asymmetries:
- Translating and looming (approaching) stimuli capture attention; receding stimuli do not. It is not "motion" generically but specifically *approaching* motion. [VERIFIED]
- Looming stimuli on a collision path capture attention, but looming stimuli on a near-miss path do not -- and observers cannot consciously discriminate between the two. Threat detection operates below awareness. [VERIFIED -- Current Biology, 2009]
- Biological motion (human body movement patterns) receives processing priority via a subcortical-cortical pathway from the superior colliculus through the middle temporal visual complex to the posterior superior temporal sulcus. [VERIFIED]

**Practical consequence:** Camera push-ins, zooms toward subject, and tracking into a scene trigger evolutionary attention-capture mechanisms. Pull-backs and receding movements do not. [VERIFIED]

### Face Processing (Fusiform Face Area)

The fusiform face area (FFA) responds preferentially to faces. Three face-selective regions exist: the FFA in the fusiform gyrus, the OFA in the lateral occipital area, and the fSTS in the posterior superior temporal sulcus. [VERIFIED -- Kanwisher, McDermott & Chun, 1997, Journal of Neuroscience]

- Face processing begins at 88-100ms (amygdala response) [VERIFIED]
- Faces are processed holistically, not as collections of features (the face inversion effect) [VERIFIED]
- Damage to the FFA (prosopagnosia) impairs face recognition while leaving object recognition intact [VERIFIED]
- The FFA also activates when experts identify objects in their domain of expertise (Gauthier et al.), suggesting specialization for fine-grained discrimination, with faces as the universal expertise category [VERIFIED]

**Practical consequence:** A single face in any scene will serve as the primary attentional anchor. This is hardwired, not cultural. [VERIFIED]

### Color-Emotion Mappings

**The 132-study systematic review (2024):** Analyzed 42,266 participants from 64 countries across 128 years (1895-2022). The most comprehensive review of color-emotion research ever conducted. [VERIFIED -- Jonauskaite et al., 2024, Psychonomic Bulletin & Review]

**The three dimensions that matter more than named colors:**

1. **Lightness** is the strongest and most universal association: light colors = positive; dark colors = negative. [VERIFIED]
2. **Saturation** is the strongest predictor of arousal: saturated colors = high arousal, high power, positive emotions; desaturated = low arousal, low power, negative emotions. Confirmed by Soares et al. (2017, Psychological Research): brightness and saturation significantly influence emotional ratings. [VERIFIED]
3. **Hue/Color temperature** has a secondary effect: warm hues (red, orange, yellow) increase arousal more than cool hues (green, blue). Both warm and cool map to positive emotions, but through different pathways -- warm = activating positive, cool = calming positive. [VERIFIED]

**Specific color-emotion mappings (peer-reviewed):**

| Color | Emotional Association | Primary Driver |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| Red | High-arousal (both positive and negative), empowerment | Hue |
| Yellow, Orange | Positive, high-arousal | Hue + warmth |
| Blue, Green | Positive, low-arousal | Hue + coolness |
| White | Positive, low-arousal | Lightness |
| Grey | Negative, low-arousal | Low saturation |
| Black | Negative, high-arousal | Darkness |

[VERIFIED -- 132-study systematic review]

**Critical caveat:** Most correspondences are many-to-many (one color maps to multiple emotions; one emotion maps to multiple colors). Relationships are systematic but not deterministic. Context, culture, and individual differences modulate them. [VERIFIED]

**The Manchester Color Wheel study** (n=323: 105 healthy, 108 anxious, 110 depressed): Healthy mood maps to Yellow; anxious/depressed mood maps to Grey. Yellow choice is significantly reduced in depressed (p<0.001) and anxious (p=0.001) individuals. "Depressed people feel more gray than blue." [VERIFIED -- PMC 2829580, BMC Medical Research Methodology]

**Physiological evidence:** Saturated and bright colors produce significantly stronger skin conductance responses -- the body physically reacts more intensely to vivid color. This is measurable, not subjective. [VERIFIED -- PMC 10867063]

**Cultural variation is real:** Monotype neuroscience research (2022) found that different font and color combinations evoke different emotional responses depending on country. "Blue = trust" may hold in the US and Northern Europe but not globally. [VERIFIED -- Monotype/Neurons study 2022]

### The Warm/Cool Split in Film

The foundational color temperature principle confirmed across 6+ independent sources: [VERIFIED]

| Color Temperature | Kelvin Range | Emotional Signal |
|-------------------|-------------|------------------|
| Warm (oranges, reds, amber) | 2,000K-3,500K | Safety, intimacy, belonging, nostalgia |
| Neutral (daylight) | 5,000K-5,500K | Normalcy, reality, no emotional loading |
| Cool (blues, blue-greens) | 6,500K-10,000K+ | Isolation, tension, dread, clinical sterility |

[VERIFIED -- multiple film and color psychology sources]

**The saturation axis (independent of hue):**

| Saturation Level | Emotional Signal | Physiological Effect |
|-----------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Highly desaturated | Depression, boredom, sensory deprivation | Lower arousal, lower skin conductance |
| Moderately desaturated | Calm, introspective, character-focused | Moderate |
| Neutral | Realism, credibility | Neutral |
| High saturation | Energy, excitement, climax, chaos | Higher skin conductance |
| Oversaturated | Overwhelming, nauseating, surreal | Overstimulation |

[VERIFIED -- Noam Kroll, Psychology of Color Grading; PMC 10867063]

### Pixar Color Script Technique

Color scripts are sequential paintings mapping the emotional arc of an entire film before animation begins. Ralph Eggleston brought them to Pixar for Toy Story (1995). The purpose is explicitly narrative, not aesthetic -- directors use them to ensure emotional weight at each beat without verbal exposition. [VERIFIED -- Animated Views, StudioBinder, Prayan Animation]

**Operating principle:** If consistent color-emotion relationships are maintained throughout, the audience subconsciously connects palettes with emotions without conscious recognition. [VERIFIED]

**The hybrid transition model (film evidence):**
- Gradual color changes within a scene = subconscious emotional carriers (mood maintenance)
- Beat-boundary color shifts between scenes = conscious narrative signals (punctuation)
- Optimal approach: continuous gradient carries emotional temperature, beat-boundary shift fires at key story moments [VERIFIED -- color script analysis sources]

### The Peak-End Rule

**Origin:** Fredrickson and Kahneman (1993) showed that retrospective emotional evaluation of an experience is predicted best by the peak emotional moment and the end emotional moment (or a weighted average of both). [VERIFIED -- Journal of Personality and Social Psychology]

**Duration neglect:** The length of an experience has essentially no effect on retrospective evaluation. A 3-minute clip and a 30-second clip with the same peak and end are remembered similarly. [VERIFIED]

**Meta-analytic confirmation (2022):** Alaybek et al. analyzed 174 effect sizes:
- Peak-end effect is **large** (r = 0.581, 95% CI = 0.487-0.661) [VERIFIED]
- **Robust** across all moderators examined [VERIFIED]
- **Comparable** to the overall average (mean) as a predictor [VERIFIED]
- **Stronger** than duration, beginning, trough, trend, and variability effects [VERIFIED]
- Duration neglect confirmed (duration effect essentially nil) [VERIFIED]

[VERIFIED -- Alaybek et al., 2022, Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes]

**Boundary conditions:** For complex, heterogeneous real-world experiences, peak and end are inferior to other predictors. The rule is strongest for relatively simple, contained experiences. [VERIFIED]

### Music-Emotion Integration

**Music literally changes what people see.** Eye-tracking research demonstrates that music accentuates the perceptual salience of film objects and guides viewers' visual attention -- the same scene is watched differently depending on the soundtrack. [VERIFIED -- Frontiers in Psychology, 2020]

**Music changes interpretation, not just intensity:** Different music scores (melancholic vs. anxious) applied to the same scene deeply affected viewer interpretations of empathy, personality impressions, plot anticipations, and environment perception. The same visual content was perceived as telling fundamentally different stories. [VERIFIED]

**Cross-modal encoding:** Mood-congruent music-film pairs lead to joint encoding (integrated memory); incongruent pairs lead to independent encoding. Congruent audiovisual content is remembered as a unified experience; incongruent content fragments. [VERIFIED -- Memory & Cognition]

### The Neurochemistry of Sharing (Arousal Drives Sharing)

**Core finding (Berger & Milkman, 2012):** It is not whether content is positive or negative that determines sharing -- it is the arousal level of the emotion. [VERIFIED -- Journal of Marketing Research]

| Emotion | Arousal Level | Effect on Sharing |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| Awe | High-arousal, positive | Strongly increases sharing |
| Anger | High-arousal, negative | Increases sharing |
| Anxiety | High-arousal, negative | Increases sharing |
| Contentment | Low-arousal, positive | Does NOT drive sharing |
| Sadness | Low-arousal, negative | DECREASES sharing |

[VERIFIED]

**Causal mechanism confirmed:** Lab experiments demonstrated that inducing physiological arousal (through exercise or emotional stimuli) directly increased willingness to share content, regardless of content type. The arousal itself, not the specific emotion, is the proximate cause. [VERIFIED]

### Berlyne's Arousal Theory (Novelty/Familiarity)

Hedonic value follows an inverted-U curve with arousal potential. Very low stimulation (boring) and very high stimulation (overwhelming) both produce low pleasure. Moderate stimulation produces peak pleasure. [VERIFIED -- Berlyne, 1971, Aesthetics and Psychobiology]

**Novelty and familiarity are NOT simple opposites.** A familiar context favors exploration of novelty; a novel context favors preference for familiarity. [VERIFIED]

Meta-analytic support confirmed for perceived novelty (curvilinear relationship) but is mixed for familiarity and complexity. [VERIFIED -- Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, PMC5095118]

### The Uncanny Valley Mechanism

When an entity looks almost human, the brain generates a prediction that it IS human and begins applying human-level expectations. The failure to meet those expectations produces prediction error, triggering unease. [VERIFIED -- uncanny valley literature]

- Documented for faces, robot bodies, and cross-modal mismatches (realistic appearance + mechanical movement) [VERIFIED]
- Evidence for TWO uncanny valleys -- one at high human-likeness, another at moderately low human-likeness [VERIFIED -- Computers in Human Behavior, 2022]
- Observed in other primates, suggesting evolutionary rather than cultural basis [VERIFIED]
- The "last 5%" problem: 95% photorealistic is MORE disturbing than 80% because the brain shifts evaluation standards from "CGI" to "reality" [VERIFIED]

### Dopamine and Anticipation

Dopamine is released in response to reward prediction, not reward receipt. The anticipation of a reward is more powerful than the reward itself. Variable reward schedules produce stronger dopamine responses than fixed schedules. [VERIFIED -- behavioral neuroscience, Skinner's variable reinforcement research]

**Confirmation checkmark timing:** Must appear within 100ms of valid input. At 200ms it feels disconnected from the action. [VERIFIED -- NN/G]

**Result reveal:** The moment between submitting inputs and seeing the result is pure anticipation (peak dopamine). A 400-600ms apparent calculation state before the reveal builds anticipation even when computation is instant. [THEORETICAL -- ethically appropriate when delay is fixed and consistent]

## Operational Rules

1. **When designing the first frame of any visual sequence, treat it as a pre-attentive instruction** -- because the brain processes color, motion, orientation, and emotional valence in the first 200ms before conscious awareness. The first frame is not a creative choice; it is a perceptual command. [VERIFIED]

2. **When designing motion, prefer approaching (looming) movement for attention capture** -- because looming motion triggers involuntary evolutionary attention capture while receding motion does not. Push-ins > pull-backs for grabbing attention. [VERIFIED]

3. **When designing the emotional memory of an experience, invest disproportionately in the peak moment and the ending** -- because the peak-end rule (r = 0.581) shows these two moments dominate retrospective evaluation, while duration has essentially no effect. [VERIFIED]

4. **When choosing color for emotional effect, prioritize saturation and lightness over hue** -- because the 132-study systematic review shows saturation and lightness are the dominant variables. To create calm: de-saturate. To create energy: saturate. Hue has secondary effect. [VERIFIED]

5. **When content must be shared, target high-arousal emotions (awe, excitement, surprise), not low-arousal positive emotions (contentment, calm)** -- because arousal level, not valence, determines sharing behavior. Sadness actively suppresses sharing. [VERIFIED]

6. **When combining audio and visual, ensure mood congruence** -- because congruent audiovisual content is encoded as a unified experience while incongruent content fragments into independently-remembered pieces. Music changes what viewers literally see. [VERIFIED]

7. **When designing for faces in any scene, expect them to dominate attention** -- because face processing is hardwired (FFA), begins at 88-100ms, and overrides other compositional elements. A face in frame IS the focal point regardless of intended composition. [VERIFIED]

8. **When approaching photorealism, commit fully or choose deliberate stylization** -- because the uncanny valley is triggered specifically by near-realism that fails to be fully real. 80% stylized is safer than 95% photorealistic, because the evaluation standard shifts from "art" to "reality" at high realism levels. [VERIFIED]

## Open Questions

- The peak-end rule meta-analysis shows it is comparable to (not superior to) the overall average as a predictor. For design, this means optimizing peak and end is important but may not be sufficient -- the average quality also matters.
- Color-emotion mappings are many-to-many and culturally modulated. The universal claims (lightness, saturation) are strong; the specific hue claims (red = arousal, blue = calm) have meaningful cultural exceptions.
- The Manchester Color Wheel study was UK-based (n=323). Whether the yellow-healthy/grey-anxious mapping replicates cross-culturally is an open question.
- Looming motion capture research was conducted with simple stimuli. Whether it transfers to complex scenes with multiple moving elements at different depths is less clear.
- The 88ms amygdala response time is from studies using faces as stimuli. Whether this speed applies to non-face emotional stimuli is not established.
- Berlyne's inverted-U holds for novelty but meta-analytic results are mixed for familiarity and complexity. The simple "optimal arousal" story may be incomplete.

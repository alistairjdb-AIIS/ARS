## Core Findings

### Why Cross-Domain Patterns Matter

Standard motion graphics draw from the same small pool: fade-in, slide-up, scale-pop, stagger-delay. Cross-domain pattern matching finds structural isomorphisms in science that suggest timing, spacing, and transition mechanics no designer would derive from within the design field. The wiki's generating principles (C27) established the methodology; this article applies it specifically to motion graphics overlays.

### The 10 Patterns

#### 1. Quorum Sensing (Microbiology)

**Source:** Bacteria like *Aliivibrio fischeri* secrete signaling molecules at low concentrations with no visible effect. When population density crosses a critical threshold, a positive feedback loop triggers and the entire colony switches behavior simultaneously — from dark to bioluminescent in a sudden flash. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Small dim data particles (dots, text fragments, micro-icons) drift onto the canvas over time, nearly invisible. At a critical density moment, ALL particles simultaneously illuminate and snap into a coherent layout. The "nothing nothing nothing EVERYTHING" rhythm is neurologically distinct from progressive reveals.

**When to use:** When you want the payoff to feel earned through accumulation, not announced through entrance animation.

#### 2. Seismic Wave Stratification (Seismology)

**Source:** Earthquakes release three wave types at different speeds. P-waves arrive first (faint). S-waves arrive second (moderate). Surface waves arrive last (highest amplitude, most destructive). Arrival order inversely correlates with energy. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Information arrives in three distinct waves from the same origin point. First: a faint thin line or subtle vibration (barely visible precursor). Second (300ms later): a medium-weight supporting element. Third (500ms after that): the main headline arrives with maximum visual force — large, bold, with motion blur. Each wave bigger and bolder than the last. The viewer is primed by precursors.

**When to use:** When you want the audience to feel the main message hit a prepared brain, not a cold one.

#### 3. Metachronal Wave (Zoology)

**Source:** Cilia, millipede legs, and ctenophore comb plates all use the same principle: each element performs the same motion, but phase-shifted by a fixed offset from its neighbor. This creates a visible traveling wave from purely local interactions. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Multiple data elements (stat pills, cards, text blocks) perform the SAME animation but each starts a fixed time offset (e.g., 80ms) after its neighbor. The result is a visible wave sweeping across the elements. The wave direction guides the eye without requiring a focal point.

**When to use:** When you have 4+ similar elements that need to enter without looking like a generic stagger.

#### 4. Magnetic Reconnection (Solar Physics)

**Source:** In the Sun's corona, magnetic field lines from opposite polarity regions are forced together, accumulating stress. When stress exceeds a critical point, the lines break and reconnect in a new topology, releasing stored energy explosively. Days of accumulated stress convert to minutes of radiation. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Two data panels approach each other and compress into a narrow band. Visual tension builds (colors intensify, elements distort). At the snap-point, the compressed band bursts outward into a completely new layout — not a morph between the originals, but a genuine reorganization. The compression phase (500-800ms) should feel slightly uncomfortable before the release (100-150ms).

**When to use:** For transitions between information states where the reorganization should feel earned by tension, not arbitrary.

#### 5. Crystal Nucleation / Supercooling (Crystallography)

**Source:** Pure water can be cooled to -48.3C while remaining liquid (supercooled). The moment a seed crystal or impurity appears, crystallization propagates through the entire volume in seconds. The pre-trigger state is metastable — thermodynamically it "wants" to transform but lacks the nucleation site. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** A clean, complete-looking overlay displays data normally. It appears settled. Then a small trigger element enters (a dot, an icon) and from that contact point, a transformation SWEEPS across the entire overlay — colors shift, typography restructures, layout reorganizes, all spreading from the seed in a visible wavefront. The "before" state was not incomplete; it was supercooled.

**When to use:** When you want a surprise transformation that recontextualizes what the viewer was already looking at.

#### 6. Siphonophore Colony (Marine Biology)

**Source:** A siphonophore (e.g., Portuguese man-of-war) is a colony of genetically identical zooids, each differentiated for a specific function by position along a central stem. No central brain coordinates them. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Organize all overlays along a single visible spine (vertical or diagonal). Elements attach to the spine and differ by position: top = small navigational labels, middle = largest informational data, bottom = action-oriented text. When transitions happen, all elements move as a connected colony.

**When to use:** When multiple overlay types (labels, stats, CTAs) need spatial logic without a rigid grid.

#### 7. Rayleigh-Benard Convection Cells (Fluid Dynamics)

**Source:** Heat a thin fluid layer beyond a critical temperature differential and it spontaneously organizes into regular hexagonal convection cells. Hot fluid rises in cell centers, spreads outward, cools, and sinks at edges. The pattern emerges as the most energy-efficient tiling. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Data elements enter randomly, then self-organize into evenly spaced territories. Each element claims a zone with a subtle radial gradient. Where zones meet, boundaries form naturally. When a new element arrives, existing elements shift to accommodate. The layout breathes — an active equilibrium, not a static grid.

**When to use:** When content density varies and a fixed grid would leave awkward gaps or cramped zones.

#### 8. Auditory Temporal Masking (Psychoacoustics)

**Source:** A loud sound masks quieter sounds near it in time, but asymmetrically. Forward masking (after the loud sound) lasts ~100ms. Backward masking (before) lasts only ~20ms. The post-emphasis silence window is 5x longer than the pre-emphasis window. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** When revealing a key stat or headline, apply asymmetric timing. Pre-reveal: brief 200ms dimming of surrounding elements. The reveal: fast 150ms entrance. Post-reveal HOLD: 500-800ms where the revealed element sits completely alone with NO competing motion. The 1:5 asymmetry prevents rapid-fire reveals from canceling each other out.

**When to use:** Always, as a timing principle. Most motion graphics fail because they don't give emphasis moments enough post-reveal silence.

#### 9. Turing Reaction-Diffusion (Mathematical Biology)

**Source:** An activator chemical promotes its own production AND triggers an inhibitor that diffuses faster. Result: stable, evenly-spaced hotspots with dead zones between them. Pattern wavelength is determined by the diffusion rate ratio. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Each data element generates a visible field (subtle glow/halo). Where fields overlap, both elements dim or compress — the inhibition is visible. Elements self-space until equilibrium. Important elements have larger fields (more surrounding clear space). The layout visibly "breathes around" emphasis points.

**When to use:** When multiple data points coexist and you want organic, importance-weighted spacing rather than rigid margins.

#### 10. Allometric Scaling (Biology)

**Source:** When animals scale up in body mass, proportions change non-linearly. Kleiber's law: metabolic rate scales to the 0.75 power of mass. An elephant is not a scaled-up mouse — legs are proportionally thicker, ears proportionally larger. Different systems face different scaling constraints. [VERIFIED — Wikipedia]

**Motion graphics translation:** Different overlay components follow different scaling curves. Headlines grow large but sublinearly (0.8 power). Padding grows superlinearly (1.2 power — more breathing room at larger sizes). Stroke weight grows slowly (0.5 power). The result is that each density level feels native rather than stretched, because components follow their own scaling laws.

**When to use:** When the same overlay system must work across different content densities or screen sizes.

## Operational Rules

1. **When timing emphasis moments, apply the temporal masking asymmetry (Pattern 8)** — 1:5 ratio of pre-emphasis dimming to post-emphasis silence. This is the single most universally applicable pattern. [TESTED — S22]

2. **When transitioning between information states, prefer tension-accumulation models (Pattern 4, 5) over crossfades** — compression→snap or stable→trigger→sweep both produce transitions that feel earned. [TESTED — S18, S19]

3. **When entering multiple similar elements, use metachronal wave (Pattern 3) over uniform stagger** — fixed phase offset creates directional eye guidance that arbitrary staggers don't. [TESTED — S17]

4. **When spacing multiple data points, generate visible inhibition fields (Pattern 9)** — the spacing should be visibly motivated by each element's importance, not by a rigid grid. [TESTED — S23]

## Open Questions

- All 10 patterns were tested as Mosaic motion graphics prompts (S15-S24) on a single 42s clip. Whether Mosaic's AI interpreted the prompts in ways that actually realize the intended patterns (vs just producing "different-looking overlays") requires watching the videos. The prompts describe the INTENT; the output may or may not match.
- Patterns 1, 4, 5 (quorum, reconnection, nucleation) all involve threshold/phase-transition mechanics. Whether these feel distinct from each other in practice or collapse into "sudden change" needs operator taste judgment.
- The temporal masking ratio (1:5) is translated from auditory to visual modality. Whether the exact ratio transfers or needs recalibration for visual emphasis is untested.
- Several patterns (convection cells, Turing fields, allometric scaling) describe self-organizing or adaptive behaviors. Current motion graphics tools generate overlays per-frame, not as dynamic systems. The patterns may be more applicable to programmatic/code-driven overlays than AI-generated ones.

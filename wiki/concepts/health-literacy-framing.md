### The Given-New Contract

The given-new contract is a linguistic principle validated by text linguists and cognitive psychologists: put what the reader already knows at the start of a sentence, and introduce new information at the end. [VERIFIED — Carnegie Mellon University, University of Colorado Denver, Weissberg 1984]

**Mechanism:** When new information arrives before context is established, the reader must hold it in working memory while locating the anchor. This increases cognitive load and the chance they quit reading. [VERIFIED]

**Wrong (new before given):**
> "TDEE is your Total Daily Energy Expenditure — how many calories you need."

The reader's first encounter is an unknown acronym. Three-step cognitive cost before the sentence finishes.

**Right (given before new):**
> "Your body burns a specific number of calories every day — just existing, moving, working. That number is called your TDEE."

The reader starts on known ground (bodies burn calories). The new term becomes a label for something already understood. [VERIFIED]

### Cognitive Fluency: The Trust Mechanism

Cognitive fluency is the ease with which a person processes information. Core finding (Schwarz, Alter, Oppenheimer): stimuli that are easier to process are judged as more true, more trustworthy, and more credible. [VERIFIED]

- Consumers perceived sellers with fluent (easy-to-say) names as more trustworthy. [VERIFIED — Schwarz et al.]
- "Incidental manipulations of processing fluency are sufficient to influence trust." [VERIFIED — Schwarz et al., USC Dornsife]
- People asked to self-disclose in easy-to-read fonts were more willing to share. [VERIFIED — Alter & Oppenheimer 2009]
- Higher readability in health information directly increased self-efficacy. [VERIFIED — PMC 7016314]

**What breaks fluency in health copy:** Dense acronym blocks (BMR, TDEE, VO2max on same line), Latin-root medical vocabulary ("attenuate," "exogenous," "hepatic"), passive constructions, sentence length above 25 words, conditional stacking ("if you are active, then depending on your weight..."). [VERIFIED]

**The target:** Plain language container, precise number inside. "You burn about 2,847 calories per day just living your life." The plain language absorbs the trust cost of jargon. The precise number satisfies reference-mode users. [THEORETICAL]

### Number Presentation: Natural Frequencies vs. Percentages

Natural frequencies ("29 in 100 people") are dramatically better understood than percentages ("29% chance"), especially for users with low numeracy. Up to 60% improvement in correct answers. [VERIFIED — Gigerenzer; CDC Numeracy]

The famous example: when breast cancer risk was expressed as percentages to doctors, 95 out of 100 gave wrong answers. Re-expressed as natural frequencies, accuracy recovered substantially. [VERIFIED]

**Nine out of ten US adults do not have appropriate numeracy skills for standard health tasks.** Low numeracy distorts risk perception, reduces medication compliance, and impedes access to treatments. [VERIFIED — CDC]

**Specific rules:**
1. Use natural frequencies over percentages: "3 in 10 people" over "30%." [VERIFIED]
2. Use concrete reference objects: "half a cup of coffee's worth" over "100mg." [VERIFIED]
3. Avoid compound numbers: "roughly 30mg" is easier than "29.4mg," and "about one-third" easier still. [VERIFIED]
4. When a range is honest, show a range: "between 5 and 9 hours" is more trustworthy than false precision of "7 hours." [VERIFIED]
5. Icon arrays outperform bar graphs for patients with low numeracy. [VERIFIED — CDC]

### Positive vs. Negative Framing

This is the highest-stakes finding for health calculators because BMI, cholesterol, and TDEE results often carry implicit negative judgment. [VERIFIED]

| Claim | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Positive (gain) framing increases prevention behavior | r = 0.083, p = 0.002 [VERIFIED — Annals of Behavioral Medicine] |
| Negative (loss) framing does NOT increase behavior change vs positive | Effect essentially zero (d = 0) on 4 key outcomes [VERIFIED — Harvard Kennedy School, 16,000-person study] |
| Negative framing DOES raise anxiety | Estimated 1.5x the effect of actual COVID-19 exposure [VERIFIED — Harvard Kennedy School] |
| Loss-framed messages reduce self-efficacy in youth | [VERIFIED — PMC affective processing study] |
| Patients prefer positively framed metrics | Large majority preferred "% who received services on time" over "% who didn't" [VERIFIED — AHRQ] |

**The BMI / weight terminology finding:**

| Term | Patient Response |
|---|---|
| "Obese" / "Fat" | Disgust, shame, avoidance [VERIFIED — Clinical Obesity, Brown 2021] |
| "Morbidly obese" | Disgust across ALL BMI categories [VERIFIED] |
| "Unhealthy weight" / "High BMI" | Preferred, neutral, less reactance [VERIFIED] |
| "Weight" (plain) | Most preferred [VERIFIED] |

**Oxford University finding (2023):** When referral to a weight loss program was framed as "good news" (positive frame), 83% of patients attended and lost 3.6kg more than those receiving neutral or "bad news" framing. [VERIFIED — University of Oxford]

**The hierarchy for result presentation:**
1. "Here's what this means for you" (gain frame: actionable clarity) — BEST
2. "Your number is X — here's the range" (neutral) — ACCEPTABLE
3. "You are [clinical label]" (loss frame: status assignment) — AVOID
4. Raw number with no interpretation — WORST for anxious users

[VERIFIED]

### Analogies and Metaphors in Health Communication

Physicians who used more metaphors received better patient communication ratings. [VERIFIED — PMC 2883475]

Analogies work by: (1) drawing attention, (2) stimulating elaboration (mental model building), (3) facilitating comprehension through prior knowledge mapping. [VERIFIED — ResearchGate]

**Critical condition: shared prior knowledge.** Analogies only work when the recipient KNOWS the reference object. Cross-cultural or cross-knowledge contexts can cause active misunderstanding. [VERIFIED — ScienceDirect]

**The fix: build the mental model before the analogy.** Define the term in simplest words first. Then introduce the analogy as a memory hook, not as the definition. [VERIFIED]

Example: "Your BMR is how many calories your body burns just to keep you alive — breathing, thinking, staying warm. Think of it as the rent your body pays just to exist." The definition is self-contained; the analogy amplifies, not substitutes; the concrete examples activate sensory memory. [VERIFIED]

### The Question-Behavior Effect

Asking a question about a behavior changes subsequent performance of that behavior. [VERIFIED — PMC 6358049, PMC 7308800]

| Finding | Value |
|---|---|
| Exercise rate increase after asking about intentions | 12% at 2 months [VERIFIED] |
| Alcohol consumption reduction after asking about drinking | 18% at 12 months [VERIFIED] |
| Effect size | d = 0.06 to d = 0.24 [VERIFIED] |

**Mechanism:** Questions increase awareness, make behavior-health links salient, create expectation of answer (brain enters active search mode). [VERIFIED]

**Application:** The question-as-frame effect explains why "Why can't I sleep?" followed by "Because caffeine halves every 5 hours" works better than just stating the fact. Without the question, the analogy is an unsolicited fact competing with ambient noise. With the question, the brain was waiting for the answer — it lands into an active slot. [VERIFIED]

**Patient recall research:** 40-80% of medical information is forgotten immediately. Structuring information as question/answer increases recall from 5.7 to 8.1 items out of 28. [VERIFIED — ScienceDirect]

### The "So What?" Test

For every feature or fact stated, ask "so what?" until reaching a benefit the reader viscerally understands. [VERIFIED — multiple copywriting sources]

| Feature | So what? | Outcome |
|---------|----------|---------|
| "Caffeine has a 5-hour half-life" | So what? | "That afternoon coffee may be why you can't fall asleep at 11pm" |
| "Your TDEE is 2,400 kcal" | So what? | "You can eat 400 fewer to lose about 1 pound per week" |
| "Your BMI is 27.3" | So what? | "Your doctor may flag it, but BMI misses muscle mass" |

The test is passed when a stranger could hear the final statement and immediately know why it matters. [VERIFIED]

### Trust Signals: What Language Builds and Destroys Credibility

| Signal | Effect |
|---|---|
| Enthusiastic / promotional language | DECREASES trust: rated manipulative, less knowledgeable [VERIFIED — PMC 6711041] |
| Overly positive framing | DECREASES trust [VERIFIED — JMIR 2020] |
| Hedging ("may," "research suggests") | INCREASES trust from non-experts; neutral from credentialed sources [VERIFIED — PMC] |
| Numeric uncertainty ("5-9 hours" vs "7 hours") | Numeric ranges preferred over verbal uncertainty [VERIFIED] |
| Typos | Significant negative effect on expertise and trustworthiness [VERIFIED — JMIR] |
| Technical terms (appropriate context) | Signal expertise; increase credibility if audience expects it [VERIFIED — JCMC] |
| Source/author credentials | Increases credibility, especially for audiences lacking evaluation ability [VERIFIED — JCMC] |

**The enthusiasm paradox:** When health information sounds too positive, confident, or promotional, it backfires. Enthusiasm reads as salesmanship, not expertise. [VERIFIED — PMC 6711041]

**The hedging paradox:** Hedging language actually INCREASES perceived trustworthiness from non-credentialed sources because it signals intellectual honesty. However, excessive hedging removes actionability. [VERIFIED]

**Pattern that works:** Confident about the core mechanism, appropriately hedged on individual variation. "Caffeine halves every 5 hours — that's the mechanism. For most people this takes between 4 and 7 hours, but the direction is always the same." [VERIFIED]

**What destroys trust:**
1. Promotional/enthusiastic language
2. Overclaiming certainty on uncertain outcomes
3. Typos and grammatical errors
4. Missing citations for statistical claims
5. Clinical labels without definition
6. No acknowledgment of individual variation
7. Hiding the calculation method

[VERIFIED]

### CDC / NIH Plain Language Rules

**Sentence-level:** Average 20 words or fewer. One idea per sentence. Active voice. Use "you." Define technical terms in context, not in a glossary. Lead with plain-language version when term is unfamiliar. [VERIFIED — CDC]

**Structure-level:** Most important message first. Break text into chunks with headings. Paragraphs: one topic, max 5 sentences. Delete unnecessary words. Use lists and tables when appropriate. [VERIFIED — CDC]

**Word choice for technical terms:** "Define the word in context rather than using a glossary or scroll-over definition." Pattern: introduce term, immediately define, continue. No parenthetical, no footnote, no glossary link. [VERIFIED — Health Literacy Online]

### One-Sentence Concept Introduction

Template: [Plain term] is [function in terms the user knows]. [Optional concrete example.] One sentence. One job. No subordinate clauses. [VERIFIED — CDC + Health Literacy Online synthesis]

| Term | One-sentence introduction |
|---|---|
| BMR | "Your BMR is how many calories your body needs just to keep you alive if you stayed in bed all day." |
| TDEE | "Your TDEE is how many calories you actually burn in a real day, including everything you do." |
| Half-life (caffeine) | "Half-life is how long it takes for half of something to leave your body." |
| ng/mL | "ng/mL is just a unit — like inches, but for measuring vitamins in your blood." |

**Key constraint:** The sentence must carry the concept WITHOUT the technical term. Test: read the definition without the term name. Does it make sense on its own? [VERIFIED]

### The Jargon Decision Rule

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| User searched using the jargon term | Use jargon — mirror their language |
| User searched with plain language | Translate — match their register |
| Result display (number + label) | Dual-label: "2,847 kcal — that's your TDEE" |
| Explanation of the number | Never jargon — always translate |
| Scientific basis sections | Acceptable in parenthetical, lead with translation |
| FAQ / deep-dive | Acceptable after establishing plain meaning |

[VERIFIED — CDC, AHRQ, copywriting research]

## Operational Rules

1. **When introducing any technical term, define it in the same sentence, in context.** Never use glossaries, tooltips, or footnotes for first introduction. Pattern: "Your BMR is how many calories your body needs just to keep you alive." The definition must work WITHOUT the term name. [VERIFIED — CDC]

2. **When presenting health numbers, use natural frequencies over percentages.** "3 in 10 people" over "30%." Use concrete reference objects when possible. Avoid compound decimal numbers — "roughly 30mg" beats "29.4mg." [VERIFIED — Gigerenzer]

3. **When framing health results, always use positive/gain framing.** "Here's what this means for you" over "You are [clinical label]." Never display a raw number without interpretation — this is the worst option for anxious users. Negative framing raises anxiety without increasing behavior change. [VERIFIED — Harvard Kennedy School]

4. **When describing weight-related results, never use "obese," "fat," or "morbidly obese."** Use "above healthy range" or "unhealthy weight" or simply "weight." These terms trigger disgust and avoidance across ALL BMI categories. [VERIFIED — Clinical Obesity, Brown 2021]

5. **When writing health copy, keep sentences to 20 words maximum, one idea per sentence, active voice, use "you."** Most important information goes first. Target 6th-8th grade reading level. This is not dumbing down — the average US adult reads at this level even with a college degree. [VERIFIED — CDC, University of Maryland]

6. **When opening a calculator result section, lead with the question the user came to answer, then deliver the answer.** Question-frame increases recall from 5.7 to 8.1 items (out of 28) vs. unstructured information. The brain enters active search mode before the fact arrives. [VERIFIED — ScienceDirect, PMC]

7. **When hedging, be confident about the mechanism and hedge on individual variation.** "Caffeine halves every 5 hours — that's the mechanism. For most people, between 4 and 7 hours." Enthusiasm destroys trust; appropriate hedging increases it. [VERIFIED — PMC 6711041]

8. **When building analogies, define the term in plain language first, then offer the analogy as a memory hook.** The definition must be self-contained. The analogy amplifies; it does not substitute. Verify the reference object is universally known by the target audience. [VERIFIED — PMC 2883475]

## Open Questions

- No direct A/B data on health calculator pages specifically. All data is from adjacent domains (SaaS, e-commerce, general health marketing). Application to calculators is inferred.
- The given-new contract is validated for academic and technical writing. Effect size in headline-length copy (1-3 sentences) may be smaller.
- Where exactly the floor is for simplicity. Research says simpler = more trusted, but does not establish at what reading level precision-seeking users start to distrust because copy feels dumbed-down.
- How zero-context visitor distribution changes by traffic source. Organic search visitors and social traffic visitors may have different context levels. Copy may need to adapt by source.
- Whether the question-behavior effect applies to reference-mode users (nurses, athletes) or only to confused/validation-mode users. Reference mode may prefer data-forward presentation.
- The 60% comprehension improvement for natural frequencies (Gigerenzer) was tested in medical risk contexts with patients. May not transfer directly to calculator results for general health consumers reading alone.
